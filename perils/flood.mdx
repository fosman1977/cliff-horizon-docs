The flood peril covers commercial operators against **extreme single-day precipitation events** that cause operational downtime through flooding. It is distinct from the rain/work-stop peril: this is not about accumulated wet days but about individual extreme events with nonlinear economic consequences.

---

## Why the GEV model, not Gumbel

Extreme precipitation tails have negative shape parameters (k < 0 in GEV notation). This means the distribution has a **heavier tail than the Gumbel** — extreme events are more likely than a Gumbel fit would predict.

CH fits a **GEV (Generalised Extreme Value)** distribution to annual maxima:

```
GEV(x; μ, σ, k)  with k ≈ −0.2 to −0.3 for Gulf/Turkey sites
```

Using Gumbel (which forces k = 0) would systematically understate the probability of events like Dubai April 2024. The GEV fit — calibrated with L-moments for robustness to outliers — correctly captures the heavy tail.

**Forward-climate loading:** The CC intensification signal (~7%/°C following Clausius-Clapeyron, corroborated by the WWA 2024 Dubai attribution study) is applied to the GEV parameters. This is where the moat is strongest: most market participants are pricing flood from historical GEV with no CC loading.

## Two-source cross-check

Every flood quote discloses both data sources and their basis:

| Source | Coverage | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| NASA POWER | Global, ~0.5° | Free, GPM-family |
| ERA5 / Open-Meteo | Global, ~31km | Free, ECMWF reanalysis |

For the Dubai April 2024 event, NASA POWER recorded **53mm** and ERA5/Open-Meteo recorded **100mm** for the same 24-hour period. Both are disclosed. The settlement source is specified in the contract — the basis is the buyer's acknowledged risk.

<Note>
Reanalysis is a floor, not a ceiling. Station observations (where available) and satellite products consistently show higher peak intensities than reanalysis gridded products. CH treats this as a conservative bias in the index.
</Note>

## Flood-line as attachment point

The flood-line (attachment point) is the precipitation threshold that triggers the downtime payout:

```
Payout = min(capDays, max(0, eventDays_above_flood_line)) × burnPerDay
```

The flood-line is a pricing dial. Key reference points for Dubai:

| Flood-line | Return period (historical) | Premium (AED m, 30d cap, AED 3m/day) |
|---|---|---|
| 25mm | ~1-in-4 years | Higher |
| 50mm | ~1-in-13 to 1-in-34 years | Sweet spot (AED 5.9–7.6m) |
| 70mm | ~1-in-34 years | Lower |
| 100mm | ~1-in-45 to 1-in-130 years | Catastrophe layer |

The **sweet spot** for buyer economics is the 50–70mm range: frequent enough to be credible, rare enough for capital to price it below the buyer's pain threshold.

---

## Derivative-only form

The flood peril is currently **derivative only**. Unlike rain/work-stop, there is no forecast-accuracy warranty form for flood because:

1. The trigger is a single-day extreme event, not an accumulation that can be forecast in advance.
2. The operational relevance for warranty buyers (who need to act on the forecast) is lower.

Ensuro is the capital wrapper in all cases.
